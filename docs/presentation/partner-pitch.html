02 / 09The Problem
Why this needs to exist

Agents are multiplying. Nothing connects them.

  • Every team is standing up agents — for coding, review, support, data — with no shared way to run, chain, or monitor them.
  • Two agents from two different vendors can't hand off a task. There's no common wire format for "here, you finish this."
  • Two agents from two different companies definitely can't. There's no discovery, no trust model, no auth pattern for that — it doesn't exist yet, anywhere.
  • So the same orchestration plumbing gets rebuilt, badly, inside every company that tries this. Nobody is solving the connective layer.

03 / 09The Idea
What A2A-Mesh actually is

The switchboard AI agents don't have yet.

Build

Define, deploy in minutes

Register an agent with a prompt, a model, a config. It comes up live behind a real A2A endpoint — no glue code.

Orchestrate

Chain agents into pipelines

One agent's output flows into the next automatically, with loops and conditions built into the engine.

Federate — the one that matters

Call another company's agents

Your agents discover and call a partner company's agents under a trust relationship you define, rate-limit, and can revoke. This is why it's a platform, not a tool.

06 / 09Why Now
Three forces landing at once

Being A2A-native today is being HTTP-native in 1995.

  • Models are finally good enough to do real work. Claude, GPT, Gemini can carry a task end to end. The gap left is orchestration and connectivity — not raw capability.
  • A2A is becoming the shared standard, not a protocol we're inventing ourselves. Google published it; major platforms are converging on it. We're building on the rails, not laying our own track.
  • Cross-company AI collaboration is untouched. Every company is building its AI stack in total isolation. Nobody has shipped the layer where two companies' agents safely talk.

This is an infrastructure bet, timed to a standard that's still being written. That window doesn't stay open long.
